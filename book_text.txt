here Flesh meets the Infinite.
The body, wrapped in flesh marks the boundary
where the physical meets the metaphysical. In a physical sense,
the body is flesh bound by skin—a dynamic parchment upon
which the story of our senses is inscribed. Some might consider
it a bag of bones, unless you’re an arthropod, then its chitin. Yet,
exceptions abound: earthworms, corals, squids. For now, let’s
focus on Homo sapiens.
We have five senses, each extending our body’s limits beyond
the skin with the exception of touch. The upper boundary of
our flesh becomes the lower boundary to the rest of the senses.
Additionally, personal space varies for each person, creating a
distance predicated on intimacy factors.
Over millennia, human senses have evolved, finely tuning
our interaction with the environment, adapting our boundaries
of perception. If we take each of the remaining senses, smell
would carry the next determinant, as aroma is agreeable, while
odor might not be. Olfaction, the technical term for our
perception of smell carries positive, neutral or negative
connotations with subtlety. Sight follows, simply because you
can hear further than you can see. For instance, if you consider
hearing a plane flying above you but not being able to see the
pilots if you’re standing on the run way during takeoff.
Additionally, you can hear reflected sounds, but you can’t see
around a corner without a mirror.
However, it’s more logical to discredit this perspective and
accept that you can see further than you can hear. For example,
you can see a distant mountain but not hear the conversation of
mountain climbers. These two sensory inputs are simply limited

he one in time.
It is often suggested that taking care of problems early
on prevents them from becoming bigger issues that require more
effort and resources to fix later. What if, however, one simply
doesn’t catch the small tear, or does so but doesn’t stitch it? A
time bomb ticking towards an imminent catastrophe?
In world systems, would the same principle apply? For us
to consider these questions, we have to understand the bigger
perspective within which they lie i.e. intentionality and
outcome. There is of course, a non-linear, complex relationship
that binds one’s conscious intention and subconscious actions.
Conventional wisdom suggests that our conscious actions,
not only play a role in shaping our intentions, but also directly
lead to corresponding outcomes. After all, it is the third Law of
a famous English polymath, stated as “Action and reaction are
equal and opposing”.
Largely undisputed, this view can instead be seen as more
nuanced with an interplay between the reaction—our stated
goal, and the action —one’s own motivation. A result of the
interconnected nature of these systems as initially introduced.
Consider the disconnect between conscious intention and
subconscious behavior. For instance, in the human body, we
might intend to eat healthy, but unconsciously yearn for comfort
foods when stressed. This is similar to the world in the case
of particular economic strategies like corporate tax cuts,
deregulation and privatization efforts as well as Quantitative
Easing (QE) and low interest rates. Well-meaning but in
practice, these lead to unintended increases in wealth inequality.
The tax cuts can result in shareholder-centric corporations
that boost stock prices, widening the gaps between the wealthy

and the average worker. When essential services are privatized,
like education and health care, the hardest hit are always the
low-income individuals as the cost of these services can increase.
Central banks have hoped to stimulate economic growth, only
to benefit the significant asset holders since they drive up the
prices of stocks, real estate and other financial assets. Rising costs
were inevitable for those that didn’t see comparable benefits.
Examples of successful failure in action include:
The United States Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) of 2017
which significantly lowered tax rates from 35% to 21% with the
goal of increasing investment and job creation. However, the
Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy reported that much
of the tax savings went toward stock buybacks rather than wage
increases or job creation, leading to greater shareholder gains.
The privatization of water in Bolivia from 1990 to 2000,
intended to improve the service efficiency, resulted in a
substantial increase in prices, affecting lower-income citizens in
a disproportional manner. The United Nations Development
Programme (UNDP) reporting prices increased by as much as
300%. This led to mass protests known as the Cochabamba
Water War.
The Quantitative Easing policy by the European Central
Bank from 2015 to 2020 raised the value of assets which in
turn benefited the wealthier Europeans over lower-income
households. Initially intended to boost economic growth but
instead led to higher living costs and decreased housing
affordability.

Outside the realm of human experiences and global systems,
a similar non-linear relationship between intention and outcome
is mirrored in the natural world. This can come as a result of
human interaction or independently.
One such example is the bioluminescent nature of
phytoplankton. On the surface, this is a spectacular visual effect
privy to certain beaches that glow at night. An outcome far
beyond its original “intention”. This classical disconnect of a
beautiful light show to the original self-defense mechanism
highlights yet another scientific phenomenon akin to the
doppler effect.
The relationship is reinforced between only two points of
view—the source and the observer —and also that of the
relativity of change in the wave frequency with motion as a
variable. In this context we have the intender, the recipient of
intention and the witnesses of the intention or the effect on
the recipient. All the complexities can be expressed as mutually
exclusive or highly interdependent events.
For many of the bioluminescent species of phytoplankton,
the self-defense mechanism works through different means
making it an effective strategy for survival. When disturbed,
emitting a flash of light can startle or confuse potential

Cognitive bias is a systematic error in thinking that affects
the decisions and judgments that people make, often as a result
of our brain’s attempt to simplify information processing. These
mental shortcuts can distort our perceptions and hence
intentions in ways that don’t align with reality. Examples include
the Dunning-Kruger effect, where people with low ability,
knowledge, or skill in particular area over estimate their own
competence.
This often stems from the lack of self-awareness to accurately
assess themselves and the overestimation of ability as a result
of inexperience or information limitation. The effect generally
diminishes with increase in proficiency and knowledgeability. Its
recognition can encourage humility and openness to learning.
Perception of outcomes and attribution to event causality
are directly influenced by cognitive biases. Systemically, the

The psychology of self-sabotage as a form of self-destructive
behavior can be manifested in different ways, often leading to

In nature, autotomy is a defense mechanism where an animal
voluntarily sheds a body part, typically to escape a predator. This
is seen in many lizard species such as geckos, which can detach
their tails when threatened. This behavior might appear
self-sabotaging at first glance, however, it often leads to
unexpected outcomes.
Immediate survival is one, as the continuous tail wiggling
distracts the predator, energy conservation while the tail is
absent, regeneration and adaptation to future attacks, and an
overall survival and evolution of the species through a
successfully adopted mechanism.

Adversity refers to a state of difficulty or misfortune that
disrupts the status quo challenging an individual, ecosystem, or
system to adapt, persevere, or evolve. It often follows a natural
pattern that is cyclical with setbacks initially manifesting as
disruptions that frequently paving the way for long-term growth.
Systems, whether biological or psychological have proven
to flourish through overcoming challenges catalyzed by the
interplay found between pain and progress. As such, adversity as
a precursor to significant development should be synonymous.
A common thread can be woven through man and nature as
immediate pain meets resilience and growth.
Emotional distress can stem from failure or loss within the
contexts of personal development, athletic achievement or career
transitions, which one might consider as immediate pain.
However, the ultimate contribution to growth as a driving force
is through reassessment of trajectories that lead to development
of new skills, which align better with one’s passions and
strengths.
A perfect example is Steve Jobs, who, after being ousted from
the company he co-founded, founded other companies and
through his experiences enabled him to lead Apple to
unprecedented success upon his return. Have you ever faced a
setback that, in hindsight, led to personal growth?
